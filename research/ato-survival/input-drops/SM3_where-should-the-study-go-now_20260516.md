## Core transition
The study moved from a static social-media-ban article series into a live observatory for how society restructures childhood under digital restriction systems.

Original question:
- Did the social media ban work?

Better question:
- What new ecosystem emerged after the gate was installed?

Wider thesis:
- The ban is not removing children from the internet.
- It is forcing society to decide what kind of internet children are supposed to grow up inside.

## Key POVs

### 13-year-old
Theme: social survival, exclusion, workaround behaviour, belonging pressure.
Line: Adults call it protection. Kids can experience it as disappearance.

### 16-year-old
Theme: birthday unlock, reactivation, platform reacquisition, pre-warmed customer.
Line: Paused child. Preloaded customer.

### Parents
Theme: operational burden, conflict, device management, negotiation.
Line: The ban removed the app icon, not the argument.

### Teachers / schools
Theme: hidden labour, spillover, wellbeing load, social conflict migration.
Line: The law targeted platforms. Schools absorbed the fallout.

### Legislators / regulators
Theme: visible action, evidence gaps, version-one limitations.
Line: Version 1 drew the line. Version 2 must measure the system.

### Platforms / tech companies
Theme: compliance theatre, incentive economics, retained ecosystem value.
Line: Compliance may be cheaper than reform.

### Media
Theme: moral clarity, workaround blindness, narrative simplification.
Line: The headline is the ban. The story is the workaround.

### Global copycats
Theme: exported policy, copied headlines, missing lessons.
Line: Australia built the first gate. Others may copy it before we know where the kids went.
